# Assignment Content

Evaluates instructional quality, structure, narrative style, and content patterns in generated assignment.md files. Each criterion focuses on how the content serves the learner -- whether they can follow the reasoning, find information in a scannable structure, and build understanding through well-placed context.

## Explains why not just what

Evaluate whether every concept answers not just "what" but also "why", "when to use it", and "what happens if you don't." Learners should understand the reasoning behind each action.

Score 4: Each concept or step explains why with a specific rationale tied to the task or technology. Learners understand the purpose behind each action.
Score 5: Every concept is motivated with concrete reasoning, real-world use cases, and consequences of alternatives. Learners understand not just what to do but why this approach was chosen over others.
Score 3: Brief reasons are given but they are shallow and generic (e.g., "this is a best practice") without connecting to the learner's context.
Score 2: One-line descriptions accompany steps but provide no reasoning. The learner follows instructions blindly.
Score 1: Bare list of commands or steps with no explanation. The learner has no idea why any action matters.

## Assignment structure

Evaluate whether assignments follow a consistent structure: introduction (context setting), concept explanation, hands-on steps, and reinforcement (summary or connection to next challenge).

Score 4: Consistent structure across assignments. Each section serves its purpose -- introduction sets context, steps are clearly delineated, and the assignment closes with a summary or bridge to the next challenge.
Score 5: Clear, consistent, and well-paced structure throughout. Each section flows naturally into the next. Transitions are smooth and the reader never feels lost.
Score 3: A recognizable flow exists but transitions between sections are abrupt. The structure is present but not polished.
Score 2: Some structure is present but assignments are missing an introduction or skip explanation before steps.
Score 1: No discernible structure. Content jumps randomly between concepts, commands, and explanations.

## Narrative vs bullets

Evaluate the balance between narrative prose and bullet/numbered lists. Explanations and context should use narrative prose. Commands, options, and reference items should use lists.

Score 4: Natural balance where prose explains concepts and lists organize actionable or reference content. The format serves comprehension.
Score 5: Deliberately effective. Engaging prose for explanations and scannable lists for actions. The format actively aids comprehension -- a reader can skim lists for commands and slow down for prose explanations.
Score 3: Both formats are present but content leans too heavily toward one style, reducing readability.
Score 2: Both formats are used but in the wrong places (e.g., bullet points for explanations, prose paragraphs for command lists).
Score 1: Content is entirely bullet points or entirely dense paragraphs with no variation.

## Real world context

Evaluate whether practical real-world scenarios reinforce learning. Context should appear where it aids understanding, not forced into every paragraph.

Score 4: Real-world context is present where it adds value and absent where the concept is self-evident. Not indiscriminate.
Score 5: Strategic use throughout. Present where it reinforces understanding, absent where the concept is clear on its own. Scenarios feel authentic, not contrived.
Score 3: Real-world context is present for some concepts but missing where it would significantly help understanding.
Score 2: One or two passing references to real-world use but no developed scenarios.
Score 1: No real-world context where concepts clearly need framing to be meaningful.

## Term introduction

Evaluate whether technical terms are explained on first use, abbreviations spelled out before being used as shorthand, and terminology used consistently afterward.

Score 4: All technical terms are defined on first use and abbreviations are spelled out. Usage is consistent afterward.
Score 5: Every term includes a clear plain-language explanation on first use. Abbreviations are spelled out. Terminology is consistent throughout with no drift or synonyms that could confuse.
Score 3: Most terms are defined, but one or two are used before being introduced.
Score 2: Some terms are defined but others are used without introduction.
Score 1: Jargon and abbreviations are used without definition throughout.

## Callouts

Evaluate the use of callout blocks (notes, warnings, tips). These should appear where learners are likely to be confused or make mistakes.

Score 4: Callouts are placed at common struggle points and contain actionable, specific guidance.
Score 5: Callouts are strategically placed at every likely confusion point. Each is actionable and specific. No unnecessary callouts dilute their impact.
Score 3: Callouts appear at some key points but miss common trouble spots.
Score 2: Callouts exist but are placed randomly, not at likely confusion points.
Score 1: No callouts anywhere in the assignment.

## Content matches audience level

Evaluate whether generated content matches the stated audience skill level from the plan.

Score 4: Consistently matches the stated level. Explanations are appropriately detailed for the audience -- not over-explaining for experts or under-explaining for beginners.
Score 5: Every section is precisely calibrated to the stated audience. Explanation depth varies intelligently -- new concepts get full treatment, prerequisite concepts get confident references.
Score 3: Generally matches, but one or two sections are noticeably above or below level without acknowledgment.
Score 2: Multiple sections are misaligned with the target skill level.
Score 1: Pervasive mismatch between content difficulty and stated audience level.

## Assignment manages cognitive load

Evaluate whether generated assignments introduce one concept at a time with immediate practice.

Score 4: One concept followed by immediate practice throughout. Information is progressively disclosed rather than front-loaded.
Score 5: Every concept is individually introduced and immediately practiced. Natural alternation between explanation and hands-on work. No section overwhelms the learner.
Score 3: Generally one concept with practice, but one or two sections pack too much before hands-on work.
Score 2: Mostly one concept at a time, but several sections introduce two or more concepts before practice.
Score 1: Multiple concepts introduced simultaneously. Theory is front-loaded before any practice.
