# Assignment Flow

Evaluates whether the planned assignment outline tells a coherent story within the challenge. Good assignment flow means each step builds on the previous one -- concepts are introduced before they are used, the challenge has a clear arc from introduction to practice, and cognitive load is managed by introducing one concept at a time.

## Assignment flow and progression

Evaluate whether the assignment outline follows a logical sequence where each step builds on the previous.

Score 4: Each step builds on the previous. Concepts are introduced before they are used. The challenge has a clear arc from introduction through practice.
Score 5: Tight narrative flow -- introduction sets context, concept sections build incrementally, hands-on steps apply what was just taught, reinforcement closes the loop. A reader could not suggest a better step order.
Score 3: Clear flow from introduction to practice, with minor sequencing issues -- one concept may appear slightly before its foundation is laid.
Score 2: Some logical order but the flow has gaps. Concepts appear before they are explained, or the connection between steps is unclear.
Score 1: Steps are disconnected topics with no logical order. The assignment reads as a collection of unrelated instructions.

## Cognitive load management

Evaluate whether the plan introduces one concept at a time with immediate practice, rather than front-loading theory.

Score 4: One concept followed by immediate practice throughout. Progressive disclosure is planned -- information arrives when the learner needs it, not before.
Score 5: Every concept is individually introduced and immediately practiced. The plan shows deliberate alternation between explanation and hands-on work. No section introduces more than one new concept.
Score 3: Generally one concept per section, but one or two sections pack multiple concepts before any hands-on work.
Score 2: Mostly one concept at a time, but several sections plan to introduce two or more concepts before practice.
Score 1: Multiple concepts introduced simultaneously. Theory is front-loaded before any practice. The plan asks learners to hold too much in working memory.

## Step sequencing

Evaluate whether the planned steps within the assignment follow a natural execution order and avoid back-tracking.

Score 4: Steps follow the order a practitioner would naturally execute them. No step requires undoing or revisiting work from a previous step. Dependencies between steps are satisfied in order.
Score 5: Steps mirror the real-world workflow exactly. The sequence teaches the learner a repeatable process they could follow independently. Each step's output becomes the next step's input naturally.
Score 3: Steps are mostly sequential but one requires the learner to revisit or modify something done two or more steps earlier without clear reason.
Score 2: Steps jump between topics or locations. The learner must context-switch frequently between unrelated tasks.
Score 1: Steps are in an arbitrary order. The learner must mentally reorder the work to make sense of it.
