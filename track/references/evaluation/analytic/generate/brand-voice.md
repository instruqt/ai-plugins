# Brand Voice

Evaluates whether generated track content matches the customer's brand voice, tone, terminology, and writing style. This rubric also covers writing anti-patterns (minimizing language, passive voice) since these are fundamentally tone and voice concerns. Compare against any available customer style guide or documentation.

## Tone matches customer

Evaluate whether the track content tone matches the customer's documentation style. Look at formality level, technical depth, and personality of the writing.

Score 4: Closely matches across formality, technical depth, and personality. Reads naturally alongside customer's own content.
Score 5: Indistinguishable from the customer's own writing. A subject-matter expert familiar with the brand would not detect a difference.
Score 3: Acceptable but generic. Could be any vendor's documentation. Nothing wrong, but nothing distinctly on-brand either.
Score 2: Right general direction but noticeably off. A reader would sense something is different from the customer's usual docs.
Score 1: Tone is clearly wrong for the customer -- casual when customer is formal, or stiff when customer is friendly. Obvious mismatch.

## Voice consistency

Evaluate whether the track maintains a consistent voice across all assignments. Check for shifts in pronoun usage, contractions, sentence length patterns, and overall register.

Score 4: Consistent voice across all assignments. No jarring shifts. Reads as a unified document.
Score 5: Perfectly uniform. Reads as if written by a single author in one sitting. Pronoun usage, contractions, sentence rhythm, and tone are identical throughout.
Score 3: Consistent within individual assignments, but minor drift detectable between challenges.
Score 2: Mostly consistent but three or more noticeable shifts in voice across the track.
Score 1: Dramatic shifts between challenges -- one uses casual contractions, the next uses formal constructions. Reads like multiple authors with no editorial pass.

## Terminology correctness

Evaluate whether the track uses customer-preferred terminology consistently. Look for generic or incorrect term substitutions where customer-specific terms should appear.

Score 4: All customer-preferred terms used correctly. No generic substitutions where customer terms exist.
Score 5: Flawless terminology. Matches customer glossary exactly, including edge cases and less common terms.
Score 3: Most terms correct, but occasional generic or incorrect terms remain where the customer has a preferred alternative.
Score 2: Some customer terms present but multiple incorrect substitutions (e.g., using "server" when the customer says "node").
Score 1: Generic or incorrect terms used throughout. No evidence of customer terminology adoption.

## Product names

Evaluate whether product names use exact capitalization, spacing, and formatting as defined by the customer. Check body text, headings, code comments, CLI references, and UI element names.

Score 4: All product names correct everywhere -- body, headers, comments, and references.
Score 5: Correct everywhere including edge cases such as possessives, plurals, abbreviations, and inline code references.
Score 3: Correct in body text but inconsistent in headers, code comments, or other secondary locations.
Score 2: Most names correct but two or more instances are wrong (e.g., "Github" instead of "GitHub").
Score 1: Product names consistently wrong -- incorrect capitalization, spacing, or naming throughout.

## No anti-patterns

Evaluate whether the content avoids writing anti-patterns: minimizing words like "simply", "just", "obviously", "easy"; excessive passive voice; and other language that undermines learner confidence or clarity.

Score 4: No minimizing language. Active voice is used consistently. Language respects the learner's effort.
Score 5: Completely free of all anti-patterns. Language is consistently direct, specific, and confident without hedging or minimizing. Strong active verbs replace weak constructions throughout.
Score 3: One or two instances of minimizing language, or occasional unnecessary passive voice.
Score 2: Several instances of minimizing language or frequent passive voice that weakens the instructional tone.
Score 1: Anti-patterns are pervasive -- found on most assignments, multiple types present.
